The Capital Stack

The capital stack represents the hierarchy of a company's funding sources, from the safest to the riskiest. In a bankruptcy, claims are paid from the top down. Click on each layer to see its characteristics and associated agreements.

Super Priority / Debtor-in-Possession (DIP) Financing

This is a special type of financing for companies in bankruptcy (Chapter 11). It is granted "super-priority" status by a court, meaning it must be repaid before all other pre-existing debts, even senior secured lenders. This is done to incentivize new lenders to provide capital to keep the distressed company operating.

Key Characteristics:

  • Priority: Highest possible claim on assets.
  • Context: Used during bankruptcy proceedings.
  • Purpose: Provides liquidity for restructuring.

Typical Agreements:

DIP Credit Agreement Security Agreement

Asset-Based Lending (ABL)

ABL is a type of senior secured financing where the loan amount is determined by the value of specific current assets, such as accounts receivable and inventory. It's common for companies in retail, distribution, and manufacturing with significant working capital assets. The "borrowing base" fluctuates with the value of the collateral.

Key Characteristics:

  • Collateral: Primarily accounts receivable and inventory.
  • Structure: Often a revolving line of credit.
  • Monitoring: Lenders closely monitor collateral value.

Typical Agreements:

ABL Credit Agreement Security Agreement UCC Filings

Senior Secured Debt

This form of debt is backed by a pledge of specific collateral (assets) from the borrower. In case of default, these lenders have the first claim on those assets. This seniority and security reduce risk, typically resulting in lower interest rates compared to unsecured debt. It can be structured as term loans, notes, or bonds.

Key Characteristics:

  • Collateral: Backed by specific assets (e.g., property, equipment, IP).
  • Priority: Paid before unsecured creditors from collateral proceeds.
  • Cost: Generally lower interest rates.

Typical Agreements:

Credit Agreement (LSTA/LMA) Security Agreement Intercreditor Agreement

Senior Unsecured Debt

Unsecured debt is not backed by any specific collateral. Lenders are relying on the overall creditworthiness and cash flow of the company for repayment. In a liquidation, they are paid from remaining assets only after all secured creditors have been satisfied from their collateral. This higher risk commands higher interest rates.

Key Characteristics:

  • Collateral: None. Backed only by the company's promise to pay.
  • Priority: Below all secured debt.
  • Cost: Higher interest rates to compensate for risk.

Typical Instruments:

Notes Bonds

Subordinated Debt

Also known as junior debt, this ranks below other, more senior loans or securities. Its claim on assets is "subordinated" to senior debt. It is riskier than senior debt and therefore pays a higher rate of interest. It is often used to finance acquisitions or for growth capital.

Key Characteristics:

  • Priority: Explicitly ranks below senior debt classes.
  • Risk: High risk of non-payment in distress scenarios.
  • Return: High interest rates.

Typical Instruments:

High-Yield Bonds Mezzanine Debt

Equity

Equity represents ownership in a company (e.g., common stock). Equity holders have a claim on the company's profits (dividends) and assets, but only after all debt holders have been paid. This makes it the riskiest position in the capital stack, but it also has the highest potential for return through capital appreciation.

Key Characteristics:

  • Priority: Last in line for payment.
  • Return: Potentially unlimited through stock price growth and dividends.
  • Control: Often comes with voting rights.

Typical Instruments:

Common Stock Preferred Stock

Core Agreements & Frameworks

These legal documents form the bedrock of lending and trading relationships. Use the tabs and expandable sections below to dive deep into the purpose, common clauses, and underwriting standards of each key agreement.

Credit Agreement (Senior Secured)

The master document for most corporate loans, establishing the core lending relationship. It governs every aspect of the loan's lifecycle: funding, interest payments, operating rules (covenants), and remedies for default.

Common Clauses & Negotiation Points

  • "EBITDA" Definition: The most heavily negotiated definition. Borrowers push for "add-backs" for anticipated cost savings and non-recurring items to inflate EBITDA, making it easier to meet financial covenants.
  • Negative Covenants ("Baskets"): The core negotiation is around the exceptions, or "baskets," that allow the borrower to incur additional debt, make payments to equity holders, or sell assets.
  • "Material Adverse Effect" (MAE): Defines what constitutes a severe negative event allowing lenders to refuse funding or call a default. Lenders want a broad definition; borrowers want it narrow.
  • "Yank-a-Bank": Gives the borrower the right to forcibly prepay and remove a single non-consenting lender from the syndicate, preventing a holdout from blocking amendments.

Underwriting Standards (What Lenders Look For)

  • Leverage Ratio (Debt/EBITDA): The primary metric. Lenders typically look for a ratio of 3.0x to 5.0x depending on the industry.
  • Interest Coverage Ratio (EBITDA/Interest Expense): A minimum ratio of at least 2.5x is often required to ensure cash flow can comfortably cover interest payments.
  • Collateral Quality: An independent appraisal determines the "Net Orderly Liquidation Value" (NOLV) of collateral, and the loan amount will be a percentage of this value.
  • Management & Sponsor: The quality and track record of the management team and private equity sponsor are critical qualitative factors.

Borrower's Review Checklist

  • Are the "baskets" for debt, liens, and restricted payments large enough for our 3-5 year business plan?
  • Is there at least a 15-20% cushion between our projections and the required financial covenant levels?
  • Can our finance team realistically produce the required financial reports on time?
  • Are we required to prepay the loan with excess cash flow? Can we make voluntary prepayments without penalty?

Intercreditor Agreement

The "rulebook" for how different groups of creditors will behave towards each other when they share a common borrower and collateral. It contractually overrides statutory priority rules to establish a clear pecking order.

Key Negotiation Points (Senior vs. Junior)

  • Standstill Period: Prohibits the junior lender from taking enforcement action for a set period (e.g., 180 days), giving the senior lender exclusive control over a workout strategy.
  • DIP Financing Waivers: Senior lenders demand that junior lenders waive their right to object to a DIP financing provided by the senior lender in bankruptcy.
  • Purchase Option: The junior lender's key defensive right to buy out the senior lenders' entire claim at par upon a default, allowing them to take control.
  • Release of Liens: Junior lender must agree that its liens are automatically released if the senior lender forecloses on the collateral.

Junior Lender's Checklist

  • Is the standstill period limited and does it terminate if seniors aren't actively pursuing remedies?
  • Is our right to purchase the senior debt clear and exercisable for a reasonable period?
  • Do we have the right to cure payment defaults on the senior debt to prevent an acceleration?
  • How extensive are the bankruptcy rights we are waiving? Have we retained the right to vote on a plan?

Other Key Documents

Security Agreement

The contract that grants a lender a "security interest" in specific assets pledged as collateral. It gives the lender the right to seize and sell those assets if the borrower defaults.

UCC Filings (Uniform Commercial Code)

In the US, a UCC-1 financing statement is a public notice filed with a state office to "perfect" the security interest. This public filing puts other creditors on notice of the existing claim.

ISDA Master Agreement & CSA

The framework governing derivatives trading. The ISDA creates a "single agreement" that nets all transactions into one close-out amount upon default. The Credit Support Annex (CSA) is the collateral agreement that mitigates this net exposure.

Common Clauses & Negotiation Points

  • Automatic Early Termination: If elected, all transactions terminate automatically upon a bankruptcy filing, ensuring an immediate close-out.
  • Additional Termination Events (ATEs): Bespoke events allowing for early exit, such as a counterparty's credit rating falling below a certain level.
  • Threshold (in the CSA): The amount of unsecured exposure one party is willing to have. A strong company negotiates a high threshold; a weaker credit has a zero threshold.
  • Eligible Collateral (in the CSA): What can be posted as collateral. Standard is cash, but parties can negotiate to include government bonds (subject to a "haircut").

Common Hedging Instruments

Interest Rate Caps

An insurance policy for borrowers with floating-rate debt. The buyer pays a premium, and in return, the seller makes payments if the floating rate rises above a pre-agreed "strike rate," effectively capping the borrower's interest cost.

FX Swaps

An agreement to exchange two different currencies at an initial date and reverse that exchange at a future date. Used to manage short-term currency mismatches or hedge foreign exchange risk.
