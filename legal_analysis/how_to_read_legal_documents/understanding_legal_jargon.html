Demystifying Legal Jargon in Credit Agreements & Financial Documents

Legal documents, especially credit agreements, are filled with specialized terminology and phrases that can be confusing. This page aims to explain some common legal jargon in plain English, drawing from the "Leveraged Lending: A Comprehensive Legal Knowledge Base" (referred to as "Source Document" hereafter) where applicable.

Common Phrases and Concepts:

Notwithstanding
Meaning: In spite of; despite. This word signals that the provision it introduces takes precedence over another provision that might conflict with it. It carves out an exception or an overriding rule.
Example: "The Borrower shall not incur any Indebtedness; notwithstanding the foregoing, the Borrower may incur Permitted Indebtedness as defined in Section X." (The permission to incur Permitted Indebtedness overrides the general prohibition).
Provided that / Provided, however
Meaning: Introduces a condition, exception, limitation, or proviso to the main statement. The main statement is true or permissible only if the condition that follows is met.
Example: "The Borrower may sell assets, provided that such sale is for fair market value and at least 75% of the consideration is in cash." (The sale is allowed, but only under these conditions).
For the avoidance of doubt
Meaning: To make something clear or explicit, even if it might seem obvious or is implied elsewhere. It's used to prevent misinterpretation or ambiguity regarding a specific point.
Example: "This covenant applies to the Borrower and its Restricted Subsidiaries. For the avoidance of doubt, Unrestricted Subsidiaries are not subject to this covenant."
Without prejudice to
Meaning: Without harming, affecting, or diminishing a right, claim, or other matter. It means that one statement, action, or right does not negate or weaken another existing right or position.
Example: "The Agent may exercise any of its rights under this Agreement, without prejudice to any other rights or remedies available to it under applicable law or other finance documents."
Mutatis Mutandis
Meaning: (Latin) "With the necessary changes having been made" or "the necessary changes having been made." It's a way of applying a provision, definition, or set of rules from one context to another by making obvious or logical adjustments, without restating the whole text.
Example: "The obligations of the Guarantors under Article V (Representations and Warranties) shall apply mutatis mutandis to any Additional Guarantor acceding to this Agreement." (Meaning the R&Ws apply to the new guarantor with appropriate changes like its name, jurisdiction, etc.)
Pari Passu
Meaning: (Latin) "On an equal footing" or "with equal step." In finance, it means that claims (e.g., two series of notes, or lenders in the same tranche) rank equally in right of payment or security priority. No one claimant has precedence over the others in that group.
Example: "The Obligations of the Borrower under the finance documents rank at least pari passu with all its other unsecured and unsubordinated indebtedness." (As per a typical representation, Source Document: Section II.C).

Herein / Hereof / Hereunder / Hereby
Meaning: These words refer to the document itself in which they appear.
  • Herein: "in this document."
  • Hereof: "of this document." (e.g., "the terms hereof" means "the terms of this document").
  • Hereunder: "under this document." (e.g., "obligations hereunder" means "obligations arising under this document").
  • Hereby: "by means of this document." (e.g., "the parties hereby agree").
Example: "The obligations of the Borrower hereunder shall be absolute and unconditional."
Save as / Except as / Unless otherwise
Meaning: These phrases introduce an exception or qualification to a general statement or rule.
Example: "Save as expressly provided in Section 7.02 (Permitted Liens), the Borrower shall not create any Lien on its assets."
To the best of its knowledge (and belief), after due inquiry
Meaning: This phrase qualifies a representation or warranty. It means the statement is true based on what the party making the statement currently knows (actual knowledge) or reasonably should know after making appropriate investigations ("due inquiry"). It limits liability if something previously unknown (despite reasonable efforts to find out) later proves the statement false. The "and belief" part can sometimes soften it further to what the party genuinely believes.
Example: "To the best of its knowledge, after due inquiry, the Borrower is not aware of any pending litigation that could reasonably be expected to have a Material Adverse Effect." (Source Document: Section II.C - discussion of qualifications to representations).
Source Document: Section II.C
Material Adverse Effect (MAE) / Material Adverse Change (MAC)
Meaning: A defined term referring to a significant negative impact on the borrower's business, assets, operations, financial condition, or ability to perform its obligations under the finance documents. The precise definition is heavily negotiated and crucial.
Example: Often used as a condition precedent ("no MAC since last financials") or an Event of Default ("an event occurs that has a MAC"). Invoking a MAC clause is notoriously difficult. (Source Document: Section II.C, II.E).
Source Document: Section II.C, II.E
Pro Forma Basis
Meaning: A method of calculation that adjusts historical financial figures to reflect the assumed occurrence of a specific transaction (e.g., an acquisition, disposal, debt incurrence) as if it had happened at the beginning of the relevant testing period. Used extensively for testing incurrence covenants.
Example: "The Borrower may incur additional Indebtedness if, on a pro forma basis after giving effect to such Indebtedness, the Leverage Ratio is no greater than 4.00:1.00." (Source Document: Section II.D.2, II.D.3 - incurrence covenants context).
Shall / Will / May / Must
Meaning:
  • Shall / Must: Indicates a mandatory obligation or requirement. "Shall" is traditional legal drafting for an imperative.
  • Will: Often indicates a future mandatory action or outcome, but sometimes used less strictly than "shall." Context is key.
  • May: Indicates permission or discretion; something is allowed but not required.
Example: "The Borrower shall deliver its financial statements within 90 days." (Mandatory) "The Lender may request additional information." (Discretionary)
