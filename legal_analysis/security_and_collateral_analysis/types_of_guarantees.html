Understanding Guarantees in Leveraged Loan Security Packages

Guarantees are a vital component of the security package in leveraged lending. They are contractual promises made by third parties (Guarantors) – typically other companies within the borrower's corporate group – to satisfy the borrower's obligations under the loan agreement if the borrower itself fails to do so. This enhances the lenders' credit support beyond just the borrower entity.

Primary Source: Section II.F (Guarantees) of the "Leveraged Lending: A Comprehensive Legal Knowledge Base" (Source Document).

1. Upstream Guarantee
An upstream guarantee is provided by a **subsidiary company** for the debt obligations of its **parent company** (where the parent is the borrower or another obligor higher up the corporate structure).
Key Legal Considerations (Source Document: Section II.F):
  • Corporate Benefit: This is a primary concern. The subsidiary providing the guarantee must derive sufficient corporate benefit from the transaction that benefits its parent. If the subsidiary does not receive adequate direct or indirect benefit, the guarantee could be challenged as being prejudicial to the subsidiary's own creditors or minority shareholders, potentially rendering it unenforceable or subject to being set aside in insolvency (e.g., as a transaction at an undervalue). This is a complex legal issue varying by jurisdiction.
  • Financial Assistance: In many jurisdictions (though rules have been relaxed in some, like the UK for private companies), there are prohibitions against a company giving financial assistance (directly or indirectly) for the purpose of an acquisition of its own shares or the shares of its parent company. Upstream guarantees given in the context of acquisition finance (LBOs) need careful structuring to avoid breaching these rules.
  • Solvency of the Subsidiary: The guarantee should not be given at a time when the subsidiary is insolvent or if giving the guarantee would render it insolvent. This relates to fraudulent conveyance or preference risks.
  • Limitations/Caps: Upstream guarantees are often subject to limitations (e.g., capped at a percentage of the subsidiary's net assets, or limited by available distributable reserves) to protect the subsidiary's own creditors and solvency.

Source Document: Section II.F

2. Downstream Guarantee
A downstream guarantee is provided by a **parent company** for the debt obligations of its **subsidiary** (where the subsidiary is the borrower).
Key Legal Considerations (Source Document: Section II.F):
  • Generally less problematic from a corporate benefit perspective, as the parent company typically benefits from the financial health, success, and proper financing of its subsidiary. The subsidiary's ability to obtain financing is usually seen as beneficial to the parent's investment.
  • The value and creditworthiness of the downstream guarantee depend entirely on the financial strength, asset base, and any prior ranking liabilities of the parent company providing it.

Source Document: Section II.F

3. Cross-Stream Guarantee
A cross-stream guarantee is provided by one **subsidiary company** for the debt obligations of another **subsidiary company** (a "sister" company) within the same corporate group (i.e., subsidiaries of a common parent).
Key Legal Considerations (Source Document: Section II.F):
  • Similar to upstream guarantees, establishing sufficient **corporate benefit** for the guaranteeing sister company is crucial. This can often be argued if the companies are part of an integrated group where the financial health and operational success of one directly or indirectly benefits the others (e.g., shared services, intercompany trading, common reliance on group reputation or funding).
  • The value depends on the financial strength of the guaranteeing sister company.
  • Solvency and potential prejudice to the guaranteeing subsidiary's own creditors are also relevant concerns, potentially leading to limitations or caps.

Source Document: Section II.F

Guarantor Coverage

Guarantor Coverage Test
To ensure that a significant portion of the borrower's consolidated group provides credit support for the loan, credit agreements (especially in leveraged finance) often include a "Guarantor Coverage Test." This affirmative covenant typically requires that the borrower and the existing guarantors (taken together) must at all times represent a certain minimum percentage (e.g., 80-85%, but can vary significantly based on negotiation and group structure) of the entire group's consolidated:
  • Total Assets, and/or
  • Total Revenue, and/or
  • EBITDA.
If the group structure changes (e.g., through acquisitions of new material subsidiaries, or disposals that reduce coverage from existing guarantors, or if a guarantor is released for other permitted reasons) such that the coverage falls below the required threshold, the borrower is usually obligated to procure that new qualifying subsidiaries accede as additional guarantors (and grant security, if applicable) within a specified timeframe to bring the coverage back up to the required level.
Key Considerations:
  • The specific financial metrics (Assets, Revenue, EBITDA, or a combination) and the percentage thresholds are key negotiated points.
  • The definition of the "Group" for this test (e.g., does it include unrestricted subsidiaries? Usually not) is important.
  • There may be agreed exclusions for certain types of subsidiaries from being required to provide guarantees (e.g., immaterial subsidiaries below a certain size, subsidiaries in specific jurisdictions where providing guarantees is legally problematic, excessively costly, or would result in adverse tax consequences; regulated entities; certain joint ventures).

General Points on Guarantees:

  • Nature of Obligation: Guarantees are typically "payment" guarantees (a primary obligation of the guarantor to pay if the borrower does not, sometimes referred to as a "guarantee and indemnity") rather than "collection" guarantees (guaranteeing payment only after lenders have exhausted all remedies against the primary borrower).
  • Continuing Guarantees: They are usually drafted as continuing obligations covering all amounts due and to become due under the finance documents, both present and future, until all such obligations are fully and irrevocably discharged (the "Discharge Date").
  • Joint and Several Liability: If there are multiple guarantors, they are often "jointly and severally" liable. This means lenders can claim the full amount of the guaranteed obligations from any one guarantor, or any combination of them, up to the guaranteed amount (subject to any individual limitations), regardless of that guarantor's individual "share" of the group's overall business or assets.
  • Defenses to Guarantees: Guarantors may have certain legal defenses that could, in some circumstances, render a guarantee unenforceable or limit its scope (e.g., lack of corporate power or authority, lack of corporate benefit, unlawful financial assistance, duress, undue influence, misrepresentation by the beneficiary, material alteration of the underlying guaranteed debt without guarantor consent, discharge of the principal debtor). Lenders and their counsel work to mitigate these risks through careful drafting, due diligence (including review of corporate authorisations), and obtaining appropriate corporate approvals and legal opinions.
  • **Subordination of Guarantor's Rights (Rights of Subrogation and Contribution):** Any claims the guarantor might have against the primary borrower (e.g., for reimbursement if the guarantor pays under the guarantee – right of subrogation) or against other co-guarantors (right of contribution) are typically subordinated to the claims of the lenders against the borrower and other obligors until the lenders have been paid in full. Guarantors often waive these rights until such time.
  • Indemnity Component: Guarantees often include an indemnity component, meaning the guarantor also indemnifies the lenders against any losses, liabilities, or costs if the underlying obligations of the borrower are or become unenforceable against the borrower for any reason (e.g., due to lack of capacity, illegality). This provides a separate, primary obligation from the guarantor.
