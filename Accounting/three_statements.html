The Three Core Financial Statements

Introduction

There are three main financial statements that companies use to report their financial performance and position: the income statement, the balance sheet, and the cash flow statement. Together, they provide a comprehensive view of a company's financial health.

1. Income Statement

The income statement, also known as the Profit & Loss (P&L) statement, shows a company's financial performance over a period of time (e.g., a quarter or a year). It is calculated as follows:

Revenues - Expenses = Net Income

It tells the story of a company's profitability during that period.

2. Balance Sheet

The balance sheet presents a snapshot of a company's financial position at a specific point in time. It is based on the fundamental accounting equation:

Assets = Liabilities + Equity

This means a company's resources (assets) are funded by either debt (liabilities) or its owners' capital (equity).

3. Cash Flow Statement

The cash flow statement (CFS) shows how a company's cash has changed over a period of time. It reconciles the net income (from the income statement) to the actual change in cash (on the balance sheet). It is divided into three sections:

  • Cash Flow from Operating Activities: Cash generated from a company's normal business operations.
  • Cash Flow from Investing Activities: Cash used for or generated from investments in assets (like property, plant, and equipment).
  • Cash Flow from Financing Activities: Cash used for or generated from financing activities, such as issuing debt or equity, or paying dividends.

How the Three Statements are Linked

The three financial statements are intrinsically linked:

  • Net income from the income statement is the starting point for the cash flow from operating activities section on the cash flow statement.
  • Net income (less any dividends paid) also flows into the Retained Earnings account within Equity on the balance sheet.
  • The ending cash balance on the cash flow statement becomes the cash balance on the balance sheet for that period.
  • Changes in balance sheet accounts, such as working capital (e.g., accounts receivable, inventory), directly impact the cash flow statement.
